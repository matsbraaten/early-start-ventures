Stablecoin News & Regulation

Stablecoins — digital tokens designed to hold a steady value, usually pegged to a national currency — moved from the regulatory sidelines to centre stage in 2025–2026. Below is a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction summary of where the rules stand.

DIGEST LAST REVIEWED: JULY 7, 2026

Jump to a jurisdiction

Canada United States United Kingdom Switzerland European Union

Canada

Law passed — rules in development

Canada enacted its first federal stablecoin law in March 2026, handing supervision of stablecoin issuers to the Bank of Canada. Detailed regulations are being written now, with the regime expected to take full effect in 2027.

March 26, 2026

Stablecoin Act becomes law under Bill C-15

The Budget Implementation Act, 2025 (Bill C-15) received Royal Assent, enacting Canada's Stablecoin Act — the country's first comprehensive framework for fiat-backed stablecoins. The Act expands the Bank of Canada's mandate to include registering and supervising stablecoin issuers, and it applies to both domestic and foreign issuers whose tokens reach Canadians.

Sources: Fasken · Department of Finance Canada

2026 (ongoing)

What the framework requires

Non-bank issuers must register with the Bank of Canada, disclosing corporate ownership, financial health, and the technology behind their token. The Act requires reserves backing every token (details to come in regulations) and explicitly prohibits paying interest to stablecoin holders. Banks and other prudentially regulated financial institutions are carved out, as they are already supervised.

Sources: MLT Aikins · Osler

Expected 2027

Coming into force

The Bank of Canada has signalled 12–18 months of preparatory work from early 2026 — drafting regulations, building a registry, and setting supervisory policy — with the framework expected to come into force in 2027. Industry groups such as the Canadian Web3 Council have broadly welcomed the clarity while debating details like the interest prohibition.

Sources: Canadian Web3 Council · CIGI

United States

Law passed — agencies writing rules

The GENIUS Act, signed in July 2025, made the US the first major economy with a dedicated federal stablecoin statute. Through 2026, federal agencies have been racing to issue the implementing rules before the law takes effect.

February 25, 2026

OCC proposes rules for federally licensed issuers

The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency issued proposed rules implementing the GENIUS Act for OCC-licensed payment stablecoin issuers, covering application requirements, limits on permissible activities, a prohibition on paying interest to holders, reserve requirements, redemption obligations, and risk-management standards.

Sources: OCC · Sullivan & Cromwell

April 7, 2026

FDIC approves its own proposed framework

The FDIC approved a proposed rule establishing the prudential framework for FDIC-supervised stablecoin issuers and insured banks, addressing reserve assets, redemption rights, capital, and risk management. In parallel, the Treasury Department (with FinCEN and OFAC) proposed anti-money-laundering and sanctions-compliance rules, and separately proposed the principles for deciding when a state's regime is "substantially similar" enough to let issuers stay state-supervised.

Sources: FDIC · US Treasury · Consumer Finance Monitor

Key dates ahead

Final rules due mid-2026; regime live by early 2027

The statute requires implementing regulations within one year of enactment — by July 18, 2026. The GENIUS Act itself takes effect on the earlier of January 18, 2027 (18 months after enactment) or 120 days after the primary federal regulators finalize their rules.

Sources: Congress.gov · Morgan Lewis

United Kingdom

Draft rules out — consultation open

The UK is building a two-tier system: the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) will regulate all UK-issued stablecoins, while the Bank of England adds a second layer of oversight for stablecoins that become "systemic" — big enough to matter to financial stability.

June 2026

Bank of England publishes draft rules for systemic stablecoins

Following its November 2025 consultation, the Bank of England released a policy statement and draft rules for sterling-denominated systemic stablecoins. Notable features: a temporary issuance cap ("guardrail") of £40 billion per systemic stablecoin, and permission to hold up to 70% of backing assets in short-term UK government debt (raised from the 60% originally proposed). Feedback is due by September 22, 2026, with the Code of Practice to be finalized by end of 2026.

Sources: Bank of England · Lewis Silkin

July 2026

Bank and FCA set out how joint regulation will work

The Bank of England and the FCA published a joint approach document explaining the hand-off between them: the FCA regulates all qualifying stablecoins issued from the UK and their retail payment use; if a stablecoin grows widely used in payments and poses financial-stability risks, its issuer is brought into joint Bank/FCA regulation.

Sources: Bank of England / FCA · Payment Expert

October 25, 2027

Full FCA crypto regime goes live

The FCA's full regime takes effect on October 25, 2027, from which point crypto trading platforms, custodians, stablecoin issuers, and staking intermediaries will all require FCA authorisation to serve UK customers.

Sources: IG UK · Lewis Silkin

Switzerland

New framework proposed

Switzerland has long handled stablecoins under general banking and anti-money-laundering law rather than a dedicated statute. That is changing: a proposed framework creates a new class of licensed "payment institutions" with exclusive rights to issue regulated stablecoins.

Consultation closed February 6, 2026

A dedicated stablecoin licensing regime takes shape

Under the proposal, issuing a "Regulated Stablecoin" would be reserved exclusively for a new category of licensed Payment Institutions. Banks could not issue stablecoins directly — they would need a separately licensed entity. The new payment-institution licence replaces the old fintech licence and removes its CHF 100 million deposit cap. The revised framework is expected to take effect in late 2026 or early 2027, with a transition period.

Sources: Deloitte Switzerland · Chambers Fintech 2026

2026

FINMA to keep an official list; white papers required

The regulator FINMA would maintain a public list of Regulated Stablecoins, and issuers would have to publish a white paper — similar to a prospectus — describing the token's characteristics and risks. This brings Swiss practice into line with the EU's MiCA regime and other international frameworks.

Sources: PwC Switzerland · Global Legal Insights

April–May 2026

Tighter anti-money-laundering rules for crypto

Revisions to FINMA's Anti-Money Laundering Ordinance expanded the crypto activities covered, lowered identity-verification thresholds for crypto transactions, added enhanced due-diligence triggers for privacy coins and mixers, and strengthened beneficial-ownership documentation — responding to criticisms from the FATF's most recent evaluation of Switzerland.

Sources: FinCrime Central · Sumsub

European Union

Rules fully in force

The EU moved first with its Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), whose stablecoin rules have applied since mid-2024. In 2026 the training wheels came off: the transitional period ended, making full licensing mandatory across the bloc.

June 30, 2026

MiCA transitional period ends — licence now mandatory

The grandfathering period for crypto firms ended on June 30, 2026. ESMA confirmed in a public statement on June 23, 2026 that any entity offering crypto-asset services in the EU without a valid MiCA licence is now in breach of EU law. Stablecoin issuers have needed authorisation since June 2024; this milestone completes the rollout for the wider industry serving them.

Sources: ESMA · InnReg MiCA Guide

June 2026

Compliant euro stablecoins double in a year

Eight euro-denominated stablecoins now meet MiCA requirements, up from five at the start of the year, with combined market capitalisation up 128% year-over-year to roughly $674 million. Circle's EURC leads at about $430 million; newcomers include EUROP (Schuman Financial), EURQ (Quantoz Payments), EURI (Banking Circle), and EURAU (AllUnity).

Sources: Cryptonomist

March 2, 2026

EBA closes the PSD2/MiCA gap

The European Banking Authority's no-action period covering the overlap between payment-services law (PSD2) and MiCA for e-money tokens ended on March 2, 2026. An EBA opinion set supervisory priorities for national authorities on authorisation and the conditions for continued stablecoin transactions. Through the year, ESMA and the EBA have also issued more than a dozen technical standards clarifying white-paper requirements and reserve audits.

Sources: European Banking Authority · Scorechain
